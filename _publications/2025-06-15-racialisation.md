Over the past decade, Germany has become embroiled in several concurrent refugee crises. Under the leadership of successive, mainstream governments, Germany took on a large share of responsibility for refugee arrivals to the European Union (EU), with Germany’s government eventually succumbing to confused messaging on migration and mixed legislative approaches toward arriving refugees. This dissertation explores 21st -century German belonging through the matrix of German public opinion, quantitative indicators and policy responses during two refugee crises – the 2015 Syrian crisis and the 2022 Ukrainian crisis. The project examines the dimensions of belonging in Germany, including racial, religious, economic and gender-based prejudices, to understand constructs of inclusion in Germany more broadly. The project adopts a mixed-methods, interpretivist approach, drawing on interviews with expert voices across the academic, political and civil spheres of society, quantitative data analyses challenging dominant anti-migrant narratives and discourse analyses across mainstream and alternative media spaces. Taken together, these methodological choices allow me to trace how policy, discourse and aesthetics operate in tandem to shape refugee reception in Germany’s political and cultural imagination.
